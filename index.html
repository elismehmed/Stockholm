Stockholm's History

Stockholm's History Stockholm, the largest city and capital of Sweden, is a vibrant and historically rich city spread across 14 islands. Located at the junction of Lake Mälaren and the Baltic Sea, Stockholm has been a central hub of Swedish culture, politics, and trade for centuries.

The city's origins date back to 1252 when Birger Jarl fortified the area to protect Sweden from sea invasions. Its strategic position made Stockholm a pivotal trade center, particularly after forming a trade alliance with Lübeck in the Hanseatic League. By 1436, Stockholm was officially named the Swedish capital, signifying its importance in the Nordic region.

During the 16th century, under the leadership of King Gustav Vasa, Stockholm underwent a cultural and architectural renaissance. The city expanded rapidly and became a symbol of Swedish independence after liberation from Danish rule. Today, Stockholm blends its medieval charm with modern innovation, boasting landmarks like the Royal Palace, Gamla Stan (the Old Town), and cutting-edge technology districts.

Stockholm's Climate and the Best Time to Visit

Stockholm’s climate offers four distinct seasons. Summers are warm and inviting, perfect for long days exploring the city, while winters bring freezing temperatures, occasional snow, and quieter streets. Autumn paints the city in vibrant colors, and spring welcomes everyone back outdoors.

The warmest time to visit is mid-July, with average temperatures of 20-22°C. Winters (December to February) are cold, with temperatures sometimes dropping to -5°C.

By March and April, temperatures gradually rise, reaching up to 9°C in April and 16°C in May. Each season has its charm, making Stockholm a city for all weather lovers!

What to Do in Stockholm

Spread across 14 islands on Lake Malaren, Sweden’s capital embraces its rich history while energetically throwing itself into the future with dynamism, reflected in its stylish districts and the trendy island of Sodermalm, south of the center.

With so many bridges to walk or drive across and ferries to take you from point A to point B, Stockholm wants you to get out and about and explore everything it has to offer. The wooded island of Djurgarden offers families a fantastic day out, with museums and fun in equal measure.

Don’t miss the historical **Gamla Stan**, the Old Town of Stockholm, with its cobblestone streets and colorful buildings. Visit the **Royal Palace**, one of the largest in Europe, and explore the impressive **Stockholm Cathedral**, known for its stunning architecture.

For a unique experience, explore the **Vasa Ship Museum**, home to the 17th-century Vasa ship, and take one of the many **boat tours** around Stockholm’s archipelago, offering spectacular views of the city from the water.

Storkyrkan - Stockholm Cathedral

Located in the picturesque medieval Old Town, Storkyrkan Stockholm Cathedral is the mother church in Stockholm and dates back to the 1300s. The Cathedral has been at the heart of religious and political life in Sweden.

Highlights:

  • The wooden statue of St George and the Dragon
  • Vädersolstavlan ("The Sun Dog Painting")
  • 17th-century French baroque style pulpit

Did you know?

The church was officially named the Church of St. Nicholas but is now known as Storkyrkan, which means the Big Church.

The Vasa Museum

Visit the Vasa Museum and experience one of the biggest cultural attractions in Stockholm, Sweden - the warship Vasa - which sank in 1628 on its maiden voyage from Stockholm. Learn about the history of the ship through fascinating exhibitions and see the restored wreck that was salvaged from beneath the water.

Along with incredibly detailed exhibitions, you can join a guided tour and learn about the sinking of this magnificent ship, the years it took to salvage it, and then the long restoration process to turn Vasa into what we see today

Highlights:

  • The world's only preserved 17th-century ship, a unique glimpse into naval history.
  • Beautifully intricate carvings and original details on the Vasa's hull.
  • Interactive exhibitions showcasing life onboard and the science behind the ship's preservation.
  • Engaging guided tours that delve into the tragic yet fascinating story of the Vasa.

Did you know?

The battleship Vasa sank in 1628 after just sailing just over 1300m.

She lay below the surface for 333 years until being discovered by Anders Franzén and salvaged in 1961.

Some blame King Gustav II Adolf for the disaster, who ordered a large ship with too many heavy-caliber cannons for the ship’s dimensions. Others blame Master shipwright Henrik Hybertsson who was inexperienced at building ships with two gundecks.

Skansen

Set on Stockholm’s Djurgården island, Skansen boasts over 150 buildings that have been collected from all over Sweden, dismantled and reassembled here. Step back in time to meet the people who would have lived and worked in them in centuries past. There’s even a full-scale replica of a 19th-century Swedish town to explore, where traditional craftspeople ply their trades and demonstrate their skills as cobblers, tanners, glass-blowers and more, in full period costume.

Stroll through pretty gardens, visit the old-fashioned fairground with its charming swings and carousels and head to the open-air zoo to spot Nordic wildlife including brown bears, moose and otters.

Children will adore the kids’ zoo, where there’s a range of domestic animals including rabbits and guinea pigs, as well as a classic car track.

Skansen also plays host to a huge number of seasonal events and activities. Visit in summer for traditional folk dancing, plus jazz concerts every Monday through August. There's a traditional 18th-century-style Autumn harvest festival on the last weekend in September. In winter, the central square twinkles with fairy lights and the air is filled with the delicious smell of mulled wine and roast chestnuts as the popular annual Christmas market gets into full swing. Expect unique local handicrafts, festive church concerts and much dancing and merry-making around the Christmas tree.

Highlights:

  • Skansen history
  • Fun for kids
  • Christmas market

Did you know?

Skansen, founded in 1891, is the world’s oldest open-air museum.

Its creator, Artur Hazelius, aimed to preserve Swedish rural traditions and lifestyles.

Some of the buildings date back to the 14th century and were transported piece by piece to the site.

Nobel Prize Museum

The prestigious Nobel Prize is a world event and, since 1901, has been awarded to more than 950 people (and organizations) for their achievements in medicine, literature, physics, chemistry, economics and peace. Learn about the Nobel Laureates and the founder, Alfred Nobel, on a guided tour of the museum, or if you want to go at your own pace, opt for a self-guided tour with audio commentary available on your phone.

Highlights:

  • Permanent exhibition – These things changed the world
  • The museum shop
  • Bistro Nobel

Did you know?

The museum in Stockholm was founded in 2001, the same year as the Nobel Prize celebrated 100 years.

Alfred Nobel was a Swedish chemist, engineer, innovator, and entrepreneur who spoke five languages.

Nobel left 31 million SEK in his will to fund Nobel Prizes to award those of outstanding achievements in the future.

Junibacken

Junibacken is a fairytale world for children and their adults. Here you get to experience many of the environments and characters that Astrid Lindgren created and included in her books, and you will also find many other children's book heroes. They also change their exhibitions at regular intervals, to always be current.

Junibacken was inaugurated in 1996 and has since become one of Stockholm's five most visited places, with several hundred thousand visitors per year. The various exhibitions invite you to play and frolic in the world of fairy tales, and the stories are acted out like theatre. Here is also Sweden's largest selection of children's books - and an exciting fairy tale train.
